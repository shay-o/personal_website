①
Role rows get a › chevron on the right, identical in character to the section-level arrow. It is sized smaller (1rem vs 1.25rem) and starts at #ccc to visually subordinate it — you read it as "same idea, lower tier." Rotates 90° on expand, same as parent.
②
The expanded role title gets a text underline (already in the codebase at role-header[aria-expanded="true"]). Combined with the rotated chevron, two signals confirm the expanded state.
③
Zero new CSS paradigms. One 5-line addition to the existing role-header rule. Lowest implementation cost of all options.

①
▶ on the left creates a clear spatial rule: section controls live on the right (›), item controls live on the left (▶). An employer who reads the page quickly will intuitively parse the two-level hierarchy from position alone.
②
The ▶ icon at 0.625rem is subtle — it won't compete with the role title. On expand it rotates 90° (▼ effect). Good with or without hover, since the position itself is a permanent affordance.
③
Downside: the left indent of 24px slightly misaligns role titles from the section header text above, which is flush-left. Worth checking against visual polish expectations.

①
The tag label removes all ambiguity — "details ↓" is unambiguous even for users who don't associate › with expand. Inverts to filled black "close ↑" when open, giving clear state feedback.
②
The tag stacks below the date in a column, so the right-side column is: date on top, tag below. Clean visual grouping. On hover the tag border darkens to give pre-click feedback.
③
Tradeoff: the tag adds visual weight to every row, even rows the employer has no interest in expanding. More visually busy than Options 1 or 4. Best for audiences who may not be power users of accordion UI.

①
At rest the list reads as pure content — no chrome, no icons competing with the role names. Hover triggers a "details →" hint that fades in. When expanded, "close ↑" is always visible to allow collapse.
②
Works well on desktop where hover is available. On mobile (touch), the hover state never fires — there is zero affordance that items are expandable. Requires a fallback (e.g., always show the hint on mobile).
③
Best fit for an audience of desktop-first professional visitors (recruiters, hiring managers). Worst fit if any significant mobile traffic is expected.

Recommended: Option 1 — Safe Option. Adding a › chevron to role rows requires a single CSS addition, maintains perfect consistency with the existing design system, and works on all devices.
